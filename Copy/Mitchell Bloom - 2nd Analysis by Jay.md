## 1. Executive Summary

Bloom Financial helps sellers of highly appreciated assets (businesses, property, concentrated stock, crypto) defer capital gains tax at closing through a Deferred Sales Trust, an installment sale structure under Section 453 of the tax code. It costs the prospect nothing to explore, works alongside their existing CPA, and must be in place before the sale closes.

The biggest opportunity is reaching three audiences the strategy call locked in: employees holding post-IPO stock, owners selling trophy homes, and owners selling $2M to $20M businesses.

**Top strategic decision:** run everything through Instantly under the Bloom Financial brand, with curiosity-first copy that offers to send the mechanism and puts the prospect's CPA in the hero seat.

## 3. ICP to Test

**Offer:** Defer capital gains tax at the close of a $2M+ sale via a Deferred Sales Trust (Section 453 installment sale), at no cost to explore, alongside the prospect's existing CPA.

**Value lever:** Save money (keep roughly a third of the gain working instead of paying it at close).

**CTA strategy:** Reply-based. Offer to send the mechanism overview; secondary CTA is a CPA introduction. No booking links in E1.

### ICP-A: Concentrated-stock employees

**Hypothesis:** employees at companies that IPO'd in the last 5 to 10 years hold large low-basis positions and delay selling because of the tax bill. They are the most receptive and easiest to reach in bulk.

- **Titles:** current or former employees at target companies; senior individual contributors and up; also founders post-exit
- **Source:** LinkedIn Sales Navigator by employer (companies that IPO'd 2016 to 2024 with broad employee equity, e.g. NVIDIA-class appreciation stories), AI-enriched for tenure
- **Geography:** high-tax states first (CA, NY, NJ, MA, MN, HI, WI, VT, DC)
- **Exclude:** finance professionals (advisors, CPAs, wealth managers), anyone at brokerages
- **Signal angle:** long tenure at a post-IPO company = likely vested, likely holding

### ICP-B: Trophy home sellers

**Hypothesis:** owners of high-value homes in luxury markets face a roughly 30% combined hit at sale and have only been told about the 1031 (which does not apply to a primary residence) or nothing at all.

- **Source:** PropStream; filter value and equity; owner emails direct (multiple per property)
- **Markets:** Vail, Aspen, Breckenridge, Cherry Hills (CO); Beverly Hills, Montecito, Santa Barbara, Atherton, Los Altos, Malibu, Bel Air, Newport Beach, Rancho Santa Fe, San Francisco (CA); Fisher Island (FL); Sagaponack, Water Mill, Manhattan (NY)
- **Filter:** long ownership (bought decades ago = huge embedded gain), listed or recently listed preferred
- **Exclude:** corporate-owned, bank-owned, new builds

### ICP-C: Business sellers ($2M to $20M)

**Hypothesis:** owners with a business listed for sale (ideally contract-pending) are 60 to 90 days from a tax event their CPA has not solved. Sweet spot $2M to $10M where deals actually close.

- **Titles:** owner, founder, co-founder, CEO, managing member, principal, sole proprietor
- **Sources:** business-sale marketplaces (scraped listings as the signal), Crunchbase selling signals; fast-selling industries as the volume bucket: healthcare practices (dental, clinics, medical aesthetics, veterinary), construction, manufacturing, logistics, residential trades (HVAC, plumbing, electrical)
- **Company size:** under 100 employees, single-founder or family owned; no committees
- **Niche test cell:** mom-and-pop mobile home park owners (selling fast, PE buyers circling)
- **Exclude:** cannabis, PE-owned, franchises of large systems

### Pain points (poke the bear)

1. Your CPA told you to just pay the capital gains and move on. Did that sit right?
2. The 1031 clock (45 and 180 days) forces you into a property you do not even want.
3. A third of a lifetime of work goes to the state and the IRS in one afternoon.
4. You are done with toilets, trash, tenants, and maintenance, but the tax bill keeps you stuck.
5. Holding the stock because selling feels like cutting off your arm.

### Implicit objections

| Objection | Preemptive counter |
| :---- | :---- |
| My CPA would have told me about this | Most CPAs have never seen it. We work with your CPA, they stay in charge. Ask them to vet it. |
| Sounds too good to be legal | It is an installment sale under Section 453, a plain section of the tax code. We send the mechanism in writing. |
| I already have a financial team | Keep them. This adds one structure they do not offer. Your team reviews everything. |
| Is this expensive to find out | There is no cost to explore or to set the structure up. |

## 4. Cold Email Campaigns to Test

**Platform:** Instantly. Merge token `{{firstName}}` only. Spintax format `{{RANDOM | a | b | c}}`. Plain text, no links in E1, no open or link tracking, under 100 words per email. Every email ends with the compliance disclosure line (placeholder until Mitch's verbiage arrives).

**Sequence:** E1 day 0, E2 day 3, E3 day 7, E4 day 12.

**A/B coverage per campaign:** 3 subjects; body variants E1 x3 (angle test: pain vs curiosity vs proof), E2 x2, E3 x2, E4 x2. That is 9 steps, meeting the 3/2/2/2 gate. CTA spintax carries 3 options per block.

**Compliance framing rule** applied to every body: describe what the structure does (defer, schedule, review), never what the prospect will get.

E3 and E4 are asset-agnostic and shared verbatim across the three campaigns (full copy in the E3/E4 library below).

## 5. Call Notes / Recap

From the strategy call of 2026-07-14 (Jay Feldman, Jonathan Garces, Mitchell Bloom):

- Commercial property owners were the original target but are unreachable in bulk behind LLCs and trusts; brokers will not cooperate because they fear the deal busting or losing the 1031 commission.
- The pivot: stockholders (called the easiest by the LGJ data team), trophy home sellers via PropStream owner data, and business sellers via marketplace and Crunchbase signals. Mitchell approved all three on the call.
- Business deal size: minimum $2M, target $2M to $20M, closing sweet spot today $2M to $10M.
- Mechanism accuracy matters to this audience: third party business trust, seller assigns interest in the closing documents, trustee sells to the buyer, no constructive receipt, installment note (typically 10 years, renewable), full investment flexibility and liquidity, unlike irrevocable alternatives.
- Trust building: share the mechanism proactively, invite the prospect's CPA in, never pitch a secret. Mitchell will supply brochure, playbook, testimonials, and disclaimer verbiage.
- Compliance: Colorado RIA rules, nothing promissory, disclaimer on every email, no audit statistics or indemnification in cold copy, no Rob Lowe.
- Infrastructure: new Bloom Financial secondary domains (4+ weeks warmup), Instantly for sending, GoHighLevel for lead management with nurture material to follow, weekly monitoring via the LGJ dashboard.
- Open items from Mitch: playbook and brochure, testimonials, disclaimer verbiage, domain list, marketing materials (Dub videos).
